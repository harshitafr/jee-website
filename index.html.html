Electric Charge — The Fundamental Property

What is charge? Properties, quantization, conservation

▾

Electric charge is the fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field. There are two types: positive (proton) and negative (electron).

🔵 Physical Intuition
Think of charge like "electric identity." Just as mass creates gravitational attraction, charge creates electrostatic force. Unlike mass (always positive), charge can be positive or negative — opposites attract, likes repel.

Properties of Charge

PropertyStatementJEE Implication
TransferableCan be transferred from one body to another (electrons move, protons stay fixed)Only electrons are mobile in solids
Associated with massCharge cannot exist without mass; mass can exist without chargeCharging changes body's mass by m_e × n
ConservedTotal charge in an isolated system is constant; charge cannot be created or destroyedIn pair production: e⁻ + e⁺ (net = 0)
QuantizedQ = ±ne where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, n = 1,2,3…Fractional charges (quarks) don't appear in free state
InvariantValue of charge is independent of frame of reference (velocity)Unlike mass, charge doesn't change with speed
AdditiveTotal charge = algebraic sum (with signs) of all chargesQ_total = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃ + … (signed addition)
Quantization: Q = ±ne    where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
⚡ JEE Shortcuts
  • If body gains n electrons: charge = −ne (negative), mass increases by n×9.1×10⁻³¹ kg
  • If body loses n electrons: charge = +ne (positive), mass decreases
  • 1 μC = 10⁻⁶ C, 1 nC = 10⁻⁹ C, 1 pC = 10⁻¹² C
  • SI unit of charge: Coulomb (C). CGS unit: stat coulomb (esu). Dimension: [AT]
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Charge is scalar — never add charges as vectors! Q_total = algebraic sum
  • Charge of ±2/3e or ±1/3e (quarks) are not found in free state — never use them in problems
  • Conservation means net charge is constant, NOT that each individual charge is preserved
🤔 I'm Confused — Why can't charge be fractional?
Quarks (inside protons and neutrons) do have charges like +2/3e and -1/3e. But quarks are confined — they can never exist as free particles. In all JEE problems, the minimum free charge is e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. So any macroscopic charge must be an integer multiple of e. This is the quantization of charge.

Conductors, Insulators & Charging Methods

How charge flows, and how to charge a body

▾

🔵 Conductors

Valence electrons loosely bound → free to move. Examples: metals (Cu, Al, Au), human body, Earth, salt water

Key: Charge resides on SURFACE of conductor

🔴 Insulators

Electrons tightly bound → cannot move freely. Examples: glass, wood, rubber, plastic, dry air, mica

Key: Charge stays where placed (no spreading)

Three Methods of Charging

🔵 Charging by Friction (Triboelectric Effect)
When two bodies are rubbed together, electrons transfer from one to the other. The body that loses electrons becomes positive, the body that gains electrons becomes negative.

Examples: Glass rod rubbed with silk (glass becomes +, silk becomes −). Ebonite rod with fur (ebonite becomes −, fur becomes +).

⚠️ Key Points
Both bodies charge equally and oppositely. Total charge remains zero (conservation). A metallic rod held in hand CANNOT be charged by friction (charge flows to earth through hand).
🔵 Charging by Conduction (Contact)
A charged conductor touches an uncharged conductor. Charge distributes between them. Both conductors end up with the same sign of charge.

Distribution depends on capacitance. For identical spheres: charge splits equally. Q_each = Q_total / 2.

⚡ JEE Trick
Two identical spheres in contact: Q₁' = Q₂' = (Q₁ + Q₂)/2. If Q₁ = +6μC, Q₂ = −2μC → each gets +2μC after contact.
🔵 Charging by Induction
A charged body brought near a neutral body induces opposite charge on near side, same sign on far side. No contact needed. Charged body does NOT lose charge.
Steps for charging sphere by induction:
  1. Bring positive rod near (but not touching) sphere → near face becomes −, far face becomes +
  2. Earth the sphere (connect to ground) → + charges flow to earth, sphere now has only − charges
  3. Remove earthing wire first, then remove rod → sphere permanently −charged
⚠️ Critical: Order Matters!
Always remove earthing BEFORE removing the inducing rod. If you remove rod first, charges redistribute and sphere becomes neutral again.

Coulomb's Law — The Foundation

Force between charges, vector form, superposition

▾

The electrostatic force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the square of distance between them.

F = ke · (q₁q₂) / r²    where ke = 1/4πε₀ = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²·C⁻²
ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²·N⁻¹·m⁻²  (permittivity of free space)

Vector Form (JEE Advanced critical)

Force on charge 1 due to charge 2 (in vector notation):

F⃗₁₂ = (1/4πε₀) · (q₁q₂/r²) · r̂₁₂

where r̂₁₂ is the unit vector from charge 2 → charge 1. Newton's 3rd law: F⃗₁₂ = −F⃗₂₁

F⃗₂₁ = (q₁q₂ / 4πε₀) · (r⃗₂ − r⃗₁) / |r⃗₂ − r⃗₁|³

Coulomb vs Gravitation — Key Comparisons

FeatureCoulomb's LawNewton's Gravity
Force typeAttractive OR repulsiveAlways attractive
PropertyCharge (±)Mass (always +)
Medium dependenceYes (ε changes)No (G universal)
Relative strength~10³⁶ times stronger than gravityWeakest force
Common featuresInverse square law, central force, conservative, action-reaction pair, acts along line joining charges/masses

In a Medium (Dielectric)

F_medium = (1/4πε₀ε_r) · (q₁q₂/r²) = F_vacuum / ε_r

ε_r = relative permittivity (dielectric constant). For water: ε_r ≈ 80. For vacuum/air: ε_r = 1.

⚡ JEE Power Tip
  • Coulomb force is valid only for point charges or uniformly charged spheres (treat as point charge)
  • NOT valid for distances less than 10⁻¹⁵ m (nuclear scale)
  • The force between two charges is independent of the presence of other charges (superposition)
  • Electrostatic force is 10³⁶ times stronger than gravitational force
⚠️ Common Traps in JEE
  • Force is ALWAYS along the line joining the charges (central force)
  • Sign of force tells attraction/repulsion; use magnitude for calculations unless asked for vector
  • Coulomb's law fails for moving charges (need Lorentz force)
🤔 I'm Confused — What exactly is ε₀ and why does it appear?
ε₀ (epsilon-naught) is the permittivity of free space. It tells you how strongly the vacuum "allows" electric fields to form. A lower ε₀ would mean stronger electric forces for the same charge. The formula k = 1/4πε₀ appears because we're using SI units. In CGS, k simply equals 1. The 4π comes from solid angles in Gauss's law (makes other formulas cleaner).

Superposition Principle

Net force due to multiple charges — vector addition strategy

▾

The net electrostatic force on a charge due to a system of charges is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted by each charge independently.

F⃗_net = F⃗₁ + F⃗₂ + F⃗₃ + ... = Σᵢ F⃗ᵢ
🔵 Key Insight
The presence of other charges does NOT affect the force between any two charges. Each pairwise force is computed independently, then all are added vectorially. This is the power of superposition.

Strategy for Multi-Charge Problems

1
Identify
List all charges and find the force on the target charge due to each other charge separately.
2
Calculate magnitude
Use F = kq₁q₂/r² for each pair. Determine direction using sign of charges.
3
Resolve into components
Break each force into x and y components. Sum all x-components and all y-components.
4
Find resultant
F_net = √(Fx² + Fy²), angle θ = tan⁻¹(Fy/Fx)
⚡ Symmetry Shortcuts (JEE Favorites)
  • Equilateral triangle: Three equal charges at vertices → force on each is √3 × kq²/l² at 30° from each side
  • Square: Equal charges at 4 corners → force on each charge at corner has specific direction (use symmetry)
  • Centroid of equilateral triangle: If 3 equal charges at vertices, net force on a charge at centroid = 0 (use symmetry without calculating)
  • Equilibrium condition: A charge is in equilibrium when net force = 0. For 3 collinear charges, the middle charge is always in equilibrium if the outer charges are same sign.
JEE Trick: If n equal charges are placed at corners of a regular polygon, the net force on any one charge due to all others can be found by using symmetry — often it simplifies to zero or a specific direction.

Electric Field — The Field Concept

Definition, point charge field, field due to multiple charges

▾

Electric field at a point is the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point (test charge should be small enough not to disturb the source).

E⃗ = F⃗/q₀     |E| = kq/r² = q/(4πε₀r²)

SI unit: N/C or V/m  |  Dimension: [MLT⁻³A⁻¹]

🔵 Why do we need the field concept?
Instead of computing force every time (which requires knowing both charges), we pre-calculate the field due to the source charge. Any other charge placed there just multiplies: F = qE. This is hugely powerful for continuous charge distributions.

Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

E⃗ = (1/4πε₀) · (q/r²) · r̂
  • For positive charge: E points radially outward (away from charge)
  • For negative charge: E points radially inward (toward the charge)
  • E is a vector quantity — direction matters!

Field Due to a Group of Charges (Superposition)

E⃗_net = E⃗₁ + E⃗₂ + E⃗₃ + ... = (1/4πε₀) Σᵢ (qᵢ/rᵢ²) r̂ᵢ

Important Electric Field Results (Must Memorize)

ConfigurationLocationElectric Field
Point charge qAt distance rE = kq/r²
Infinite line charge (λ C/m)At distance rE = λ/(2πε₀r)
Infinite plane sheet (σ C/m²)Any point near itE = σ/(2ε₀)
Two parallel sheets (±σ)Between platesE = σ/ε₀
Uniformly charged ring (R, Q)On axis at xE = kQx/(x²+R²)^(3/2)
Ring maximum fieldat x = R/√2E_max = kQ/(3^(3/2)·R²/2)
Conducting sphere (Q, R)r > R (outside)E = kQ/r²

⚡ Ring Field — JEE Advanced Favorite
For ring of radius R, charge Q on axis at distance x:
E = kQx / (x² + R²)^(3/2)
  • At x = 0 (center of ring): E = 0
  • At x = R/√2: E is maximum
  • At x >> R: E ≈ kQ/x² (looks like point charge)
  • Differentiate and set dE/dx = 0 to find maximum position
🤔 I'm Confused — Why is E = 0 inside a conductor?
If E ≠ 0 inside a conductor, free electrons would experience a force and keep moving — but in electrostatic equilibrium, electrons have stopped moving. So E must be zero inside. Any net charge on a conductor resides entirely on its surface. This is also why Faraday cages work!
🔬 Advanced Insight — Field due to Disk
For a uniformly charged disk of radius R, surface charge density σ:
E = (σ/2ε₀)[1 − x/√(x² + R²)]
  • As R → ∞ (infinite plane): E = σ/2ε₀
  • As R → 0 (point charge): recovers E = kQ/x²

Electric Field Lines

Properties, patterns, and what they tell us

▾

Electric field lines are imaginary curves whose tangent at any point gives the direction of electric field at that point. They were introduced by Faraday.

12 Key Properties — All JEE-Tested

  • Field lines start from positive charges and end on negative charges (or go to infinity)
  • They never intersect each other (E cannot have two directions at one point)
  • They are not real — imaginary construct, but useful visualization
  • Number of lines ∝ magnitude of charge (more charge → more lines)
  • Denser lines → stronger field. Uniform spacing → uniform field
  • Field lines are always perpendicular to the surface of a conductor
  • No field lines exist inside a conductor (E = 0 inside)
  • Field lines do NOT form closed loops in electrostatics (only in magnetism)
  • They tend to contract longitudinally (like rubber bands — charges attract)
  • They tend to expand transversely (like repelling rubber bands — like charges repel)
  • The field at a point is tangent to the field line, not along the curved path
  • Two field lines can never be parallel at the same point in space
⚠️ Field Line Myths — JEE Trap
  • A charge does NOT move along a field line (only if starting from rest with no other forces)
  • Field lines are continuous only in empty space — they may start/end on charges
  • In a uniform field, field lines are straight parallel lines with equal spacing
⚡ Pattern Memory Aid
  • Single +q: Lines radiate outward uniformly in all directions
  • Single −q: Lines come inward uniformly from all directions
  • +q and −q (dipole): Lines go from + to −, curving around
  • Two +q: Lines repel, neutral point exists between them
  • Parallel plates: Uniform straight lines between plates

Electric Dipole

Dipole moment, field on axial/equatorial, torque, potential energy

▾

A system of two equal and opposite charges +q and −q separated by a small distance 2l. The dipole moment is:

p⃗ = q × 2l    Direction: from −q to +q

SI unit: C·m  |  Dimension: [M⁰L¹T¹A¹]

Electric Field Due to a Dipole

📍 On Axial Line (End-on position)

E_ax = 2kp / (r²−l²)²
For short dipole (r>>l):
E_ax = 2kp/r³

Direction: along p⃗ (same as dipole moment direction)

📍 On Equatorial Line (Broadside position)

E_eq = kp / (r²+l²)^(3/2)
For short dipole (r>>l):
E_eq = kp/r³

Direction: antiparallel to p⃗ (opposite to dipole moment)

Key ratio: E_axial / E_equatorial = 2 (at same distance from center, for short dipole)

Field at General Point (Short Dipole)

E = (kp/r³)√(1 + 3cos²θ)
tan α = (tan θ)/2    (α is angle of E with radial direction)

Dipole in Uniform Electric Field

  • Torque: τ⃗ = p⃗ × E⃗  →  |τ| = pE sinθ (θ is angle between p⃗ and E⃗)
  • Potential Energy: U = −p⃗·E⃗ = −pE cosθ
  • Stable equilibrium: θ = 0° (p⃗ parallel to E⃗), U = −pE (minimum)
  • Unstable equilibrium: θ = 180°, U = +pE (maximum)
  • Net force = 0 in uniform field (equal and opposite forces on ±q)
⚡ Dipole Oscillation (JEE Advanced)
If a dipole is displaced by small angle from stable equilibrium in a uniform field:
Time period T = 2π√(I/pE)    (like SHM)
where I = moment of inertia of dipole about its center. This is exactly like a bar magnet oscillating in a magnetic field!
🔬 Dipole in Non-Uniform Field
  • Net force ≠ 0 (because field is different at the two charges)
  • Force = p × (dE/dx) for 1D case
  • Dipole will also experience torque + translation
  • A dipole is attracted toward regions of stronger field
🤔 I'm Confused — Why is equatorial field antiparallel to p⃗?
Place dipole with + charge at right and − charge at left. On the equatorial line (perpendicular bisector): the field from +q points away from +q (toward the equatorial point), and field from −q points toward −q (also toward the equatorial point... which is toward −q, i.e., toward the left). The resultant points in the same direction as the field from −q, which is opposite to p⃗ (since p⃗ points from − to +, i.e., to the right). So E_equatorial is antiparallel to p⃗.

Motion of Charged Particle in Electric Field

Acceleration, projectile-like motion, uniform field dynamics

▾

A charge q of mass m in a uniform electric field E experiences force F = qE. By Newton's 2nd law:

a = qE/m      (acceleration, constant in uniform field)

Analogy with Projectile Motion

A particle entering a uniform field horizontally is exactly like projectile motion:

QuantityProjectile (gravity)Charged particle in E-field
Horizontal velocityv₀ = constv₀ = const
Vertical accelerationg downwarda = qE/m (direction depends on sign)
Vertical displacementy = ½gt²y = ½(qE/m)t²
TrajectoryParabolaParabola
⚡ Key Results
  • Time to cross plates of length L: t = L/v₀
  • Vertical deflection while in field: y = (qEL²)/(2mv₀²)
  • For particle to be in equilibrium: qE = mg → E = mg/q (electric force balances gravity)
  • For an electron in E field: a = eE/m_e ≈ very large (because m_e is tiny)
⚠️ Direction of acceleration
For positive charge: acceleration is in direction of E. For negative charge (electron): acceleration is OPPOSITE to E. Always check the sign!

Continuous Charge Distributions

Linear, surface, volume charge density — integration approach

▾

Types of Charge Distribution

Linear (λ)
dq = λ dl
Unit: C/m
Example: wire, ring
Surface (σ)
dq = σ dA
Unit: C/m²
Example: disk, plane
Volume (ρ)
dq = ρ dV
Unit: C/m³
Example: sphere

General Integration Approach

dE⃗ = (1/4πε₀) · (dq/r²) · r̂   →   E⃗ = ∫ dE⃗
⚡ Strategy for Integration Problems
  1. Choose a small element dq at position with its field dE pointing in some direction
  2. By symmetry, identify which component of dE cancels out (usually perpendicular components cancel)
  3. Integrate only the surviving component
  4. Substitute limits carefully
Ring trick: For a uniformly charged ring on its axis, the perpendicular components of dE from diametrically opposite elements cancel. Only axial component survives, giving E = kQx/(x²+R²)^(3/2).
